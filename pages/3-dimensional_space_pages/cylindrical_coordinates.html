Chapter 1.11 : Cylindrical Coordinates

Like two-dimensional space, the conventional \((x,y,z)\) coordinate system is called the Cartesian coordinate system for three-dimensional space. However, in the last two sections of this chapter, we will examine some alternative coordinate systems for three-dimensional space.

The first is the cylindrical coordinate system, a simple extension of polar coordinates into three dimensions. To achieve this extension, we add a third coordinate, \(z\), to the standard polar coordinates of \(r\) and \(\theta\).

Here is a graph of what these coordinates mean:

As you can see, the \(\theta\) measures the amount of rotation around the \(z\)-axis starting from the \(x\)-axis, \(r\) measures the radius, and \(z\) measures the elevation along the \(z\)-axis.

However, if we have a point in cylindrical coordinates, the cartesian coordinates can be found using the following conversions:

\(x = cos \left(\theta \right)\)

\(y = sin \left(\theta \right)\)

\(z = z \)

Notice that these are the same for converting \(x\) and \(y\) into polar coordinates.

We can also convert backwards, solving for \(r\), \(\theta\), and \(z\) using the following conversions:

\(\begin{align*}r & = \sqrt {{x^2} + {y^2}} \hspace{0.5in}{\mbox{OR}}\hspace{0.5in}{r^2} = {x^2} + {y^2}\\ \theta & = {\tan ^{ - 1}}\left( {\frac{y}{x}} \right)\\ z & = z\end{align*}\)
