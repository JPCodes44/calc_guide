Chapter 6.5 : Divergence Theorem

Okay, this part will explore the connection between surface and triple integrals. We'll use something called the Divergence Theorem to do this.

Divergence Theorem

Let \(E\) be a simple solid region with a positively oriented boundary \(S\). Let \(F\) be a vector field where its components contain continuous first-order partial derivatives. Then,

\(\displaystyle \iint\limits_{S}{{\vec F\centerdot d\vec S}} = \iiint\limits_{E}{{{\mathop{\rm div}\nolimits} \vec F\,dV}}\)



Let's look at some examples that utilize this theorem:

Example 1

Use the divergence theorem to evaluate \displaystyle \(\displaystyle \iint\limits_{S}{{\vec F\centerdot d\vec S}}\) where
\(\vec F = xy\,\vec i - \frac{1}{2}{y^2}\,\vec j + z\,\vec k\) and the surface consists of the three surfaces, \(z = 4 - 3{x^2} - 3{y^2}\), \(1 \leq z \geq 4\) on the top, \({x^2} + {y^2} = 1\), \(0 \le z \le 1\) on the sides and \(z = 0\) on the bottom.

Show Solution

The region \(E\) for the triple integral is then the region enclosed by these surfaces. Note that cylindrical coordinates would be a perfect coordinate system for this region. If we do that here are the limits for the ranges.

\(\begin{array}{c}0 \le z \le 4 - 3{r^2}\\ 0 \le r \le 1\\ 0 \le \theta \le 2\pi \end{array}\)

We'll also need the divergence of the vector field so let's get that.

\({\mathop{\rm div}\nolimits} \vec F = y - y + 1 = 1\)

The integral is then,

\(\begin{align*}\iint\limits_{S}{{\vec F\centerdot d\vec S}} & = \iiint\limits_{E}{{{\mathop{\rm div}\nolimits} \vec F\,dV}}\\ & = \int_{{\,0}}^{{\,2\pi }}{{\int_{{\,0}}^{{\,1}}{{\int_{{\,0}}^{{4 - 3{r^2}}}{{r\,dz}}\,dr}}\,d\theta }}\\ & = \int_{{\,0}}^{{\,2\pi }}{{\int_{{\,0}}^{{\,1}}{{4r - 3{r^3}\,dr}}\,d\theta }}\\ & = \int_{{\,0}}^{{\,2\pi }}{{\left. {\left( {2{r^2} - \frac{3}{4}{r^4}} \right)} \right|_0^1\,d\theta }}\\ & = \int_{{\,0}}^{{\,2\pi }}{{\frac{5}{4}\,d\theta }}\\ & = \frac{5}{2}\pi \end{align*}\)
