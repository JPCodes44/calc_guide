Chapter 6.3 : Surface Integrals of Vector Fields

Just like we did with line integrals, we also need to explore surface integrals of vector fields. When dealing with line integrals, the orientation of the curve we were integrating along could affect the answer. This same idea also applies to surface integrals. So, before diving into surface integrals of vector fields, we must introduce the concept of an oriented surface.

If you remember, we discovered a Mobius Strip in elementary school, a surface with only ONE side (A tangent plane on every point except the boundary). As you might have noticed, there would also have to be two normal vectors, as each point on the Mobius strip can either face up or down. Depending on the orientation of the strip, we will determine the set of the unit normal vectors that we would like to choose.

For certain types of oriented surfaces, there is a convention we must follow. To begin, we must define what we mean by a closed surface, simply a surface that forms the boundary of a solid region \(E\). A great example of a closed surface is the surface of a sphere. Now, when we talk about the orientation of a closed surface \(S\), we say it has a positive orientation when we choose a set of unit normal vectors that point outward from the region \(E\). Conversely when the normal vectors point inwards towards the region \(E\), we consider the orientation of the closed surface to be negative.

Note that the convention for \(E\) and \(S\) only applies to closed surfaces.

To determine the surface integrals of vector fields, we must write a formula for the unit normal vector corresponding with the orientation we are working with.

Let's suppose we have a function \(z=g \left(x,y \right)\). Then, we will define a new function in terms of our new function, which is the surface given by \(f \left(x,y,z \right)=0\). When dealing with a surface, paying attention to the normal vector's z-component (indicated by the k-hat vector) is important, as it's always positive and generally points upwards. This may not be straight up, but it'll have an upward component. This becomes significant when working with a closed surface and needing to determine the positive orientation. We can decide if the "positive" orientation should point up or down by looking at the normal vector. Remember that "positive" means pointing out the region, which may sometimes require a downward orientation. But if we need the downward orientation, we can take the negative of the unit vector instead. And remember, we always have the option of choosing the unit normal vector that best fits our needs.

We will leave this section with a quick interpretation of a surface integral over a vector field. If v is the velocity field of a fluid, then the surface integral \(\iint_S = F \cdot dS\) is the fluid flow rate across the surface \(S\). This is sometimes called the flux of the vector field across the surface. It is often helpful in fluid dynamics to determine the fluid flow rate across a given surface, so surface integrals can be used to help with these types of calculations.

Note that the surface integral represents the volume of fluid flowing through \(S\) per time unit (e.g. seconds or minutes or whatever unit you want to use).
