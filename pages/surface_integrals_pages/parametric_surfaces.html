Chapter 6.1 : Parametric Surfaces

Before delving into surface integrals, discussing the process of parameterizing a surface is essential. In the same way that we parameterized a curve using values of "\(t\)" from an interval \([a,b]\), we need to find a way to parameterize a surface.

To write down the parametric equations of a surface, the steps are as follows:
  1. Determine the parametric equations from the given equation
  2. Sub the parametric equations corresponding to each component in the parametric representation

To determine a surface from a parametric representation, the steps are as follows:
  1. Write down the parametric equations in terms of \(u\) and \(v\) equal to \(x\) and \(y\) (or whatever variables are in the parametric equation).
  2. Determine the surface given from those parametric equations.
  3. Write the equation for the determined quadric surface in \(x\), \(y\) or \(z\) and sub in the parametric representations (as seen in your equations)
  4. Solve for \(U\) and \(V\).
  5. Write the final equation in terms of \(x\), \(y\), and \(z\).
We will mostly be writing parametric equations of a surface rather than identifying the surface.

In general, here are the definitions for parametric representations:

\(\begin{align*}z &= f\left( {x,y} \right)\hspace{0.25in}\,\, \Rightarrow \hspace{0.25in}\,\,\vec r\left( {x,y} \right) = x\,\vec i + y\,\vec j + f\left( {x,y} \right)\vec k\\ x & = f\left( {y,z} \right)\hspace{0.25in}\,\, \Rightarrow \hspace{0.25in}\,\,\vec r\left( {y,z} \right) = f\left( {y,z} \right)\,\vec i + y\,\vec j + z\,\vec k\\ y & = f\left( {x,z} \right)\hspace{0.25in}\,\, \Rightarrow \hspace{0.25in}\,\,\vec r\left( {x,z} \right) = x\,\vec i + f\left( {x,z} \right)\,\vec j + z\,\vec k\end{align*}\)

Let's suppose we have the following proof,

Proof

Let's suppose we have the tangent plane to the parametric surface \(S\) given by,

\(\vec r\left( {u,v} \right) = x\left( {u,v} \right)\vec i + y\left( {u,v} \right)\vec j + z\left( {u,v} \right)\vec k\)

First, define

\(\begin{align*}{{\vec r}_u}\left( {u,v} \right) & = \frac{{\partial x}}{{\partial u}}\left( {u,v} \right)\vec i + \frac{{\partial y}}{{\partial u}}\left( {u,v} \right)\vec j + \frac{{\partial z}}{{\partial u}}\left( {u,v} \right)\vec k\\ {{\vec r}_v}\left( {u,v} \right) & = \frac{{\partial x}}{{\partial v}}\left( {u,v} \right)\vec i + \frac{{\partial y}}{{\partial v}}\left( {u,v} \right)\vec j + \frac{{\partial z}}{{\partial v}}\left( {u,v} \right)\vec k\end{align*}\)

If we keep the value of \(v = v_0\) fixed, then the vector \(\vec{r_u}\left(u,v_0 \right)\) will be tangent to the curve given by \(\vec{r} \left(u,v_0 \right)\) as long as \(\vec{r_u} \left(u,v_0 \right)\) is not equal to zero. Similarly, if we keep the value of \(u = u_0\) fixed, then the vector \(\vec{r_v} \left(u_0,v \right)\) will be tangent to the curve given by \(\vec{r}(u_0,v)\) as long as \(\vec{r_v} \left(u_0,v \right)\) is not equal to zero. Therefore, both \(r_u \left(u_0,v_0 \right)\) and \(\vec{r_v} \left(u_0,v_0 \right)\) (as long as neither one is the zero vector) will be tangent to the surface \(S\) given by \(\vec{r} \left(u,v \right)\) at the point \(\left(u_0,v_0 \right)\), and the tangent plane to the surface at \(\left(u_0,v_0 \right)\) will be the plane containing both \(\vec{r_u} \left(u_0,v_0 \right)\) and \(\vec{r_v} \left(u_0,v_0 \right)\).

This fact is true for any point where neither \(r_u\) nor \(r_v\) is the zero vector. Furthermore, if \(r_u \times r_v\) is not equal to zero, then the vector \(r_u \times r_v\) can be used as the normal vector to the surface \(S\). This is important because we can use the normal vector to write down the equation of a tangent plane.

Another application of parametric surfaces is calculating the surface area.

Suppose we have the parametric surface \(S\) given by,

\(\vec r\left( {u,v} \right) = x\left( {u,v} \right)\vec i + y\left( {u,v} \right)\vec j + z\left( {u,v} \right)\vec k\)

We will need to calculate the surface area of the parametric surface \(S\) by using the following formula involving \(r_u\) and \(r_v\).

\(\displaystyle A = \iint\limits_{D}{{\left\| {\,{{\vec r}_u} \times {{\vec r}_v}} \right\|\,dA}}\,\)
