Chapter 2.7 : Directional Derivatives

Defining this change is complicated because there are many ways to allow both \(x\) and \(y\) to change, such as varying at different rates or directions. To address this, we must first establish some preliminary concepts. Specifically, we must define how \(x\) and/or \(y\) change. For example, suppose we wish to find the rate of change of \(f\) at a specific point \((x_0, y_0)\), where both \(x\) and \(y\) are increasing, but \(x\) is growing twice as fast as \(y\). To define this change, we can consider a particle at \((x_0, y_0)\) moving toward increasing x and y, where the \(x\)-coordinate increases twice as fast as the \(y\)-coordinate. Then, using vectors, we can describe the direction the particle moves.

Up to this point, we have examined the partial derivatives of \(f\) with respect to \(x\) and \(y\), representing the rate of change of \(f\) as we vary \(x\) (holding \(y\) constant) and \(y\) (holding \(x\) constant), respectively. However, we must now address how to find the rate of change of \(f\) if we allow both \(x\) and \(y\) to change simultaneously.

Fact:
The gradient is normal to the level curve \(f \left(x,y \right) = k\) at the starting point \(\left(x_0,y_0\right)\). The same applies to more than three variables.
