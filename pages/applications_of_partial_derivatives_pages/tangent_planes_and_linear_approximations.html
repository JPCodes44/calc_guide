Chapter 3.1 : Tangent Planes

We saw how partial derivatives can be used as traces, but we will further extend that idea in this section.

For visualization, imagine that we have the surface of a function in \(\mathbb{R}^3\). You can think of a plane tangent to that surface as only a point.

Let us consider a point \(\left(x_0, y_0 \right)\) and denote \(C_1\) as the trace to \(f \left(x, y \right)\) for the plane \(y = y_0\) (with \(x\) varying and \(y\) fixed) and \(C_2\) as the trace to \(f \left(x, y \right)\) for the plane \(x = x_0\) (with \(y\) varying and \(x\) fixed). Then, the slope of the tangent line to \(C_1\) is \(f_x\left(x_0, y_0 \right)\), and the slope of the tangent line to \(C_2\) is \(f_y \left(x_0, y_0 \right)\).

We can define \(L_1\) as the tangent line to \(C_1\) and \(L_2\) as the tangent line to \(C_2\). The tangent plane will contain \(L_1\) and \(L_2\) while serving the same purpose as a tangent line in Calculus I. A tangent plane is a plane that touches the surface at the point and is "parallel" to the surface at that point. The tangent plane and the surface touch at \(\left(x_0, y_0 \right)\), and this point is on both the surface and the plane.

\(\left( {{x_0},{y_0},{z_0}} \right) = \left( {{x_0},{y_0},f\left( {{x_0},{y_0}} \right)} \right)\)

To obtain the equation of the tangent plane, we must define the general equation of a plane:

\(a\left( {x - {x_0}} \right) + b\left( {y - {y_0}} \right) + c\left( {z - {z_0}} \right) = 0\)

Given that \(\left(x_0,y_0,z_0 \right)\) is a point we have on the plane. We will further simplify this as follows:

\(z - {z_0} = - \frac{a}{c}\left( {x - {x_0}} \right) - \frac{b}{c}\left( {y - {y_0}} \right)\)

Also, I'm not too fond of fractions. So, as a result, we will set the fractions to CAPITAL CONSTANTS!

\(A = - \frac{a}{c}\hspace{0.25in}B = - \frac{b}{c}\)

Subbing in the constants...

\(z - {z_0} = A\left( {x - {x_0}} \right) + B\left( {y - {y_0}} \right)\)

And now, let's determine \(A\) and \(B\). Remember for traces, if we hold \(y\) fixed, then \(y_0 = y\), so the \(y\) term will cancel:

\(z - {z_0} = A\left( {x - {x_0}} \right)\)

Since this equation of the line must be tangent to the surface at \(\left(x_0,y_0 \right)\), we will assume that \(\left(y=y_0 \right)\) and \(A\) is the slope of this line.

Which is the equation for \(L_1\), and the partial derivative of \(f \left(x,y \right)\) with respect to \(x\) is the slope.

Therefore the equation is as such:

\(A = {f_x}\left( {{x_0},{y_0}} \right)\)

And if we hold x fixed at \(x = x_0\), the equation of the tangent plane becomes,

\(z - {z_0} = B\left( {y - {y_0}} \right)\)

Since this equation of the line must be tangent to the surface at \(\left(x_0,y_0 \right)\), we will assume that \(x = x_0\) and \(B\) is the slope of this line.

\(B = {f_y}\left( {{x_0},{y_0}} \right)\)

We also know that \(z_0 = f \left(x_0,y_0 \right)\), so we can write the full tangent plane equation as:

\(z - {z_0} = {f_x}\left( {{x_0},{y_0}} \right)\left( {x - {x_0}} \right) + {f_y}\left( {{x_0},{y_0}} \right)\left( {y - {y_0}} \right)\)

This equation is also known as the linear approximation because we calculate the tangent plane near the point \(\left(x_0,y_0 \right)\).

\(L\left( {x,y} \right) = f\left( {{x_0},{y_0}} \right) + {f_x}\left( {{x_0},{y_0}} \right)\left( {x - {x_0}} \right) + {f_y}\left( {{x_0},{y_0}} \right)\left( {y - {y_0}} \right)\)

If we are "near" the point \(\left(x_0, y_0 \right)\), we should have that...

\(f\left( {x,y} \right) \approx L\left( {x,y} \right) = f\left( {{x_0},{y_0}} \right) + {f_x}\left( {{x_0},{y_0}} \right)\left( {x - {x_0}} \right) + {f_y}\left( {{x_0},{y_0}} \right)\left( {y - {y_0}} \right)\)
